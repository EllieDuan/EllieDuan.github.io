About us

Dr. Ellie Duan will join the Department of Animal Science at Cornell University in July 2021 as an Assistant Professor of Functional Genomics.

Our focused on functional genomics and epigenetics in domestic animals. Our work is the first to define the X-chromosome dosage compensation dynamic in cattle embryos. Moreover, we have identified 13 novel imprinted genes that regulated fetal growth in sheep. Using WGBS, we conducted the first comprehensive analysis of the global dynamics of DNA methylation in bovine gametes and single in vivo/vitro produced embryos. Currently, Ellie Duan is a postdoctoral research associate at Department of Molecular Biology, Cell Biology & Biochemistry (MCB), Brown University, studying chromatin accessibility and transcription during zygotic genomic activation in Drosophila early embryos, in the laboratory of Dr. Erica Larschan.

Research interests

Duan Lab aim to lead an interdisciplinary and independent research program to decipher the transgenerational effect in parental-to-embryo switch. We will study the development and epigenomics from spermatogenesis and oogenesis to early embryo development. Embryos are the best model for functional genomics because their genome undergoes dramatic epigenetic reprogramming from highly differentiated gametes to pluripotent preimplantation stages. Moreover, embryos are the foundation for fetal, neonatal and adult development, performance, reproduction and longevity. Embryo loss contributes strongly to infertilities. Our research will use both the domestic animal and Drosophila model to study epigenetic regulation in early embryonic development and the reproductive system. We will also translate our findings to provide new insights that will be beneficial to both animal and human fertility and health. Our approaches will combine embryology, molecular biology, next generation sequencing, multi-omics and computational biology.

X Chromosome Dosage Compensation

I was fascinated by Ohno’s Hypothesis of sex chromosome evolution and dosage compensation which suggests that the single X-chromosome in male is transcriptionally up-regulated to equalize its gene dosage to autosomes (A). Subsequently, X chromosome inactivation (XCI) is emerged in female to achieve dosage compensation between sexes and between X and A.

Whether this hypothesis is true in mammals is still highly controversial with much international debate. However, most studies were conducted in humans, mice or other model organisms. Therefore, I decided to test Ohno’s hypothesis in domestic species to increase our current knowledge. Using embryo RNA-seq datasets, I defined the first comprehensive profile of X-chromosome up-regulation (X-to-Autosome ratio) dynamics during early embryonic development and in somatic tissues in cattle and sheep. I also identified “dosage-sensitive” genes that require complete dosage compensation.

Moreover, my work showed that X-chromosome regulation is aberrant in in vitro fertilized (IVF) embryos. Therefore, the X-chromosome plays an essential role in embryo development and has prolonged impacts on later life. To improve embryo quality and fertility, further efforts should focus on the mechanism of XCI reprogramming and X chromosome dosage compensation in embryos.

Genomic Imprinting

At UConn ANSC, there is a well-established sheep model to study trans-generational effect of maternal nutrition during pregnancy. I used this model to study the effects of maternal diets on genomic imprinting patterns and genes expression.

I developed the entire de novo bioinformatics analysis in this project. I first identified informative single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) to trace all genes’ parental origins, and then tested for their allelic expression biases. In total, I identified 13 novel imprinted genes in sheep and I validated seven genes by independent methods. Moreover, I also characterized four novel imprinted clusters. Although the maternal nutrition did not seem to change the genomic imprinting patterns, I found that maternal nutrition significantly altered imprinted gene expression levels, revealing that gene expression and genomic imprinting are regulated through different epigenetic mechanisms.

These results demonstrated that there is a trans-generational effect of maternal diet on fetal development on the epigenetic level and stressed the importance of a balanced diet during pregnancy. Further research should focus on parental effects of fetal epigenetic regulation and development.

Epigenetic dynamics in Embryos

In the last year of my Ph.D., I initiated a collaborative research project to profile the methylome in bovine single pre-implantation embryos using RNA-seq, reduced representative bisulfite sequencing (RRBS) and whole genome bisulfite sequencing (WGBS).

I preformed the entire bioinformatics analysis for the WGBS project. We found that after fertilization, the major wave of genome-wide DNA demethylation was complete at the 8-cell stage when de novo methylation became prominent. Sperm and oocytes had numerous differentially methylated regions (DMRs) which were enriched in intergenic regions. DMRs were also identified between in vivo and in vitro matured oocytes.

This is the first comprehensive analysis of the global dynamics of DNA methylation in bovine gametes and single early embryos. I provided new insight into the epigenetics of embryos produced by assisted reproduction such as in vitro fertilization as well as models for human early embryos for which studies on in vivo development are extremely limited. Future research should focus on define biomarkers that could be use in improve in vitro matured oocytes and embryos.

Zygotic Genome Activation

My postdoctoral project focuses on the role of a conserved master regulatory transcription factor (CLAMP) in regulating X chromosome dosage compensation and the maternal zygotic transition in Drosophila early embryos. I have employed diverse techniques including: classical genetics, functional genomics, chromatin mapping, bioinformatics and microscopy. Future direction will be define the underlying mechanism of early transcription factors regulating zygotic genome activation and chromatin remodeling in early embryos.
